The claim

We are here again.

Intelligence engines exist and work.
A physical theory of them does not.

■ What exists
The engines.

Biological brains. Deep networks. Neuromorphic chips. We build them, operate them, and depend on them — at ruinous energetic cost.

  • They work.
  • We have empirical scaling laws.
  • We do not know why they work.
□ What is missing
A theory of them.

A principled account of what an intelligence is, what it costs, and what any given physical substrate can efficiently do.

  • No Carnot bound.
  • No closure criterion.
  • No substrate-independent accounting.

The unifying move

One dynamics, many substrates.

Dynamical systems theory is the glue: biological, quantum, and neuromorphic learners are three physical realisations of the same operator algebra. Embodiment then imposes constraints — and constraints are where design principles come from.

— Biology
Spiking cortex
irregular spikes · sparse · cheap
— Quantum
Open-system evolution
coherent · non-commuting · lossy
— Neuromorphic
Memristive reservoir
analog state · hysteretic · embodied
— The glue
∂t ρ = 𝓛[ρ]
A single operator-algebraic representation — the same linear evolution over a lifted observable space, whatever the hardware underneath.
Constraint · 01
Thermodynamic cost
Every embodied representation pays a price in free energy. The Cost of Complexity fixes that price from three sparse assumptions — and sets a critical ceiling.
Constraint · 02
Algebraic closure
Not every substrate admits a compact local description of what it does. Equilibrium from the Inside Out gives the exact criterion for when it does.

Summary

A physics of intelligence,
made explicit.

Synthetics asks what intelligence is as a physical process, what embodied representations cost, and what different substrates can efficiently do.

One language across substrates

Price and close representations
